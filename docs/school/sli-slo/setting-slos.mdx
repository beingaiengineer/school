# Setting SLOs (Service Level Objectives)

| Difficulty | Intermediate |

## 2. Engineering Story

A passionate startup founder mandated an SLO of 100% uptime for their new microservice. Engineers spent all their time fixing minor, inconsequential bugs and building overly complex redundant systems, completely halting feature development. They soon realized that 100% is the wrong target for almost everything. Users don't need or notice perfection.

## 3. Real-World Analogy

Trying to score exactly 100/100 in every single school exam vs aiming for a solid 90% and having time for extracurricular activities and sports. Perfection is too expensive.

## 4. Concept Explanation

SLOs define the acceptable level of unreliability. They act as a balancing mechanism between the need for feature velocity and the need for system stability. By setting an SLO of 99.9% instead of 100%, you give your engineering team a 'budget' for errors, which they can spend on deploying new features or performing risky migrations.

## 5. Syntax Table

| Component | Description | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Target | The percentage goal | 99.9% |
| Window | The time period | 30 days rolling |
| SLI | The metric being tracked | HTTP 5xx errors |

## 6. Beginner Example

```yaml
# Simple SLO definition
name: Web Latency
target: 99%
condition: latency < 200ms
window: 28d
```

## 7. Real-World Engineering Example

```yaml
# Advanced SLO with multiple windows
apiVersion: openslo/v1
kind: SLO
metadata:
  name: payment-processing
spec:
  service: payments
  target: 0.9999
  timeWindow:
    - duration: 7d
      isRolling: true
    - duration: 30d
      isRolling: true
```

## 10. Top Interview Questions

| Question | Answer |
|---|---|
| Why not 100%? | Unrealistic, expensive, and limits deployment velocity. |
| Who should set the SLO? | A collaboration between Product and Engineering. |
| What is a rolling window? | A continuously moving time frame (e.g., last 30 days) for evaluation. |
| How do you improve an SLO? | By reducing errors or improving latency. |
| What happens if you consistently beat your SLO? | You might be moving too slowly. Deploy more often! |

## 11. Tricky Questions & Edge Cases

What if your internal SLO is met, but customers are complaining? Your SLI is measuring the wrong thing, or your SLO is set too low.

## 12. Real-World Usage

Stripe targets 99.999% for its core payment API, investing heavily in redundancy to achieve it.

## 13. Best Practices

| DO | DON'T |
|---|---|
| Align SLOs with business impact | Don't create SLOs in a vacuum |
| Review SLOs periodically | Don't set them and forget them |

Setting an SLO is fundamentally a business decision, not purely a technical one. It dictates how much risk a company is willing to take in pursuit of releasing new features.

The process of setting an SLO forces a healthy tension between product managers (who want to ship fast) and site reliability engineers (who want to keep the system stable). Master this negotiation, and you master modern engineering management.

Engineers must constantly evaluate the trade-offs between consistency, availability, and partition tolerance (the CAP theorem) when designing their services. No system can be perfect in all three dimensions simultaneously. Therefore, the decisions you make regarding which metrics to track and what targets to set will fundamentally shape the architecture of your application.

Furthermore, integrating these practices into your CI/CD pipelines ensures that reliability is treated as a first-class citizen, rather than an afterthought. Automated deployment checks based on current system health can prevent catastrophic rollouts and minimize the blast radius of inevitable bugs.
