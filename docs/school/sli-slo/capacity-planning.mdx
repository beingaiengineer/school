## 2. Engineering Story

A popular food delivery app crashed completely on New Year's Eve because they didn't anticipate the 10x traffic spike. The servers ran out of memory, and the database fell over. They learned the hard way to use load testing and capacity planning to forecast future resource needs based on business events.

## 4. Concept Explanation

Capacity planning is the process of determining the production capacity needed by an organization to meet changing demands for its products. In software, this means ensuring you have enough compute, memory, and network resources to handle anticipated traffic spikes, organic growth, and disaster recovery scenarios.

## 5. Syntax Table

| Strategy | Description | Use Case |
|---|---|---|
| Lead Strategy | Add capacity before demand increases | High growth startups, predictable events |
| Lag Strategy | Add capacity after demand increases | Cost-sensitive operations |
| Match Strategy | Add capacity dynamically in small increments | Cloud autoscaling |

## 10. Top Interview Questions

| Question | Answer |
|---|---|
| What is capacity planning? | Forecasting future computing resource requirements. |
| What is load testing? | Simulating heavy user traffic to identify system bottlenecks. |
| How does autoscaling help? | It dynamically adds or removes resources based on real-time metrics. |
| What is N+1 redundancy? | Having one more component than strictly necessary to handle a single failure. |
| Why not just rely on autoscaling? | Autoscaling takes time (minutes). A sudden massive spike will cause outages before new servers boot up. |

## 11. Tricky Questions & Edge Cases

Can you autoscale a monolithic relational database easily? Usually no. You scale out (horizontally) for web servers, but scaling up (vertically) a master database requires downtime.

## 12. Real-World Usage

E-commerce giants run massive distributed load tests months in advance of major shopping holidays to ensure capacity.

## 13. Best Practices

| DO | DON'T |
|---|---|
| Load test beyond expected peaks | Don't assume autoscaling solves everything |
| Plan for hardware failure (redundancy) | Don't run systems at 100% utilization |

Effective capacity planning combines technical load testing with business forecasting. You must communicate with marketing and product teams to understand upcoming promotions that will drive traffic. It's not just about adding servers; it's about understanding the breaking points of your architecture.

If you don't plan for capacity, your users will do it for you, usually at the worst possible time.

Engineers must constantly evaluate the trade-offs between consistency, availability, and partition tolerance (the CAP theorem) when designing their services. No system can be perfect in all three dimensions simultaneously. Therefore, the decisions you make regarding which metrics to track and what targets to set will fundamentally shape the architecture of your application.

Continuous learning and iteration are key. What works for a startup with thousands of users will fail spectacularly for an enterprise with millions. Adapt your strategies as your scale dictates, always keeping the end-user experience as your north star.
