## 2. Engineering Story

A major Indian e-commerce platform went down during the Big Billion Days sale. The database team claimed 99.9% uptime because their servers were responding to pings, but the checkout service was failing for 50% of users due to a misconfigured load balancer. Without clear metrics tied to user journeys, 'uptime' was a meaningless vanity metric. They needed a way to measure the actual user experience.

## 3. Real-World Analogy

## 4. Concept Explanation

SLI (Service Level Indicator) is a quantitative measure of some aspect of the level of service that is provided. SLO (Service Level Objective) is a target value or range of values for a service level that is measured by an SLI. SLA (Service Level Agreement) is an explicit or implicit contract with your users that includes consequences of meeting (or missing) the SLOs.

## 5. Syntax Table

| Term | Definition | Owner |
|---|---|---|
| SLI | The actual measurement | Engineering |
| SLO | The target for the SLI | Product & Engineering |
| SLA | The business contract | Legal & Business |

## 6. Beginner Example

```yaml
# Basic SLO definition
slo:
  metric: latency
  target: 99.9%
  threshold: <200ms
```

## 7. Real-World Engineering Example

```yaml
# OpenSLO example
apiVersion: openslo/v1
kind: SLO
metadata:
  name: checkout-availability
spec:
  service: checkout
  description: 99.9% of requests succeed
  target: 0.999
```

## 10. Top Interview Questions

| Question | Answer |
|---|---|
| Difference between SLI and SLO? | SLI is the metric, SLO is the goal. |
| Who owns the SLA? | Business and Legal teams. |
| Can you have an SLO without an SLA? | Yes, internal services have SLOs but rarely SLAs. |
| What is an error budget? | 100% minus the SLO. |
| Give an example of an SLI. | Percentage of HTTP 200 responses. |

## 11. Tricky Questions & Edge Cases

If a third-party API goes down, does it affect your SLO? Yes, if it impacts your user experience, unless you have fallback mechanisms.

## 12. Real-World Usage

Google pioneered SRE and uses SLOs extensively to manage their massive, global infrastructure.

## 13. Best Practices

| DO | DON'T |
|---|---|
| Base SLIs on user journeys | Don't base SLIs on CPU or memory |
| Keep SLOs realistic | Don't aim for 100% reliability |

## 14. Production Notes

<Admonition type="info" title="Business Alignment">

</Admonition>

## 15. Common Mistakes

| Mistake | Correction |
|---|---|
| Measuring what is easy | Measure what matters to users |
| Ignoring the SLA | Ensure your SLO is stricter than your SLA |

Understanding the distinction between these three terms is the absolute foundation of reliability engineering. Without SLIs, you are flying blind. Without SLOs, you have no destination. Without SLAs, you have no business consequences.

This foundational knowledge is critical for anyone transitioning into a Site Reliability Engineering role or a backend engineering role focused on production systems. The concepts form the bridge between technical implementation details and business objectives.

Always remember that reliability is not a binary state. A system is never just 'up' or 'down'. It exists on a spectrum of performance and availability, and these metrics are the tools you use to navigate that spectrum.

Furthermore, integrating these practices into your CI/CD pipelines ensures that reliability is treated as a first-class citizen, rather than an afterthought. Automated deployment checks based on current system health can prevent catastrophic rollouts and minimize the blast radius of inevitable bugs.

Continuous learning and iteration are key. What works for a startup with thousands of users will fail spectacularly for an enterprise with millions. Adapt your strategies as your scale dictates, always keeping the end-user experience as your north star.
