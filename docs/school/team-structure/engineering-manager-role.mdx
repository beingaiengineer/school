# The Engineering Manager Role

## 1. Quick Summary
| Area | Details |
|---|---|
| Topic | Understanding the incentives, duties, and perspective of an Engineering Manager (EM) |
| Difficulty | Beginner |
| Used For | Knowing how to manage up, negotiate promotions, and flag team blockers |
| Common Mistake | Assuming your EM is just a senior engineer who codes less |
| Performance | Time: EMs operate on a quarterly/yearly time horizon. Space: High context switching. |

## 2. Engineering Story
You are frustrated. The sprint has been a disaster. The PM keeps adding tickets, the CI/CD pipeline is broken, and a critical bug in production just wiped out your weekend. You sit down for your 1-on-1 with your Engineering Manager (EM), expecting them to dive into the codebase and fix the CI/CD pipeline with you. Instead, the EM asks, "How are you feeling about your workload?" Bro, you might think your EM is technically out of touch, but they are doing exactly what their job requires. An EM's primary job is not to write code; it is to build the team that writes the code. They are managing the system of people. If the CI/CD pipeline is broken, the EM's failure wasn't in not fixing it; their failure was in not allocating sprint capacity for a DevOps engineer to fix it.

## 4. Concept Explanation
The Engineering Manager is responsible for the **Who** and the **How** (in terms of process, not architecture). A good EM acts as a multiplier. They don't output code; they output a high-performing team. Their daily tasks involve 1-on-1s, performance reviews, hiring, firing, sprint planning, and cross-team alignment. Bro, the biggest mistake engineers make is hiding their struggles from their EM. If you are burning out, your EM needs to know immediately so they can adjust the team's velocity. Your EM is your primary advocate for promotions. If you do great work but never communicate it to your EM, they cannot effectively argue your case during the annual performance review cycle.

## 5. Syntax Table
Here is how you should communicate with your EM during 1-on-1s.

| Topic | Do NOT Say | DO Say |
|---|---|---|
| Blockers | "I'm stuck." | "I'm blocked on X because team Y hasn't delivered the API. Can you escalate to their EM?" |
| Career Growth | (Silence) | "My goal is to reach Senior level in 18 months. What gaps do you see in my current performance?" |
| Overwork | "I'll just work the weekend to finish it." | "We have too much in the sprint. Should we drop Feature A or Feature B?" |

## 6. Beginner Example
Here is a common anti-pattern for a 1-on-1 meeting.

```python
# Junior Engineer in a 1-on-1:
EM: "How was your week?"
Junior: "Good. I wrote the login controller."
EM: "Any blockers?"
Junior: "No."
# (The meeting ends in 4 minutes, and the EM has no idea the Junior is secretly miserable and wants to quit.)
```

## 7. Real-World Engineering Example
Here is how a senior engineer leverages their EM.

```python
# Senior Engineer in a 1-on-1:
def conduct_one_on_one(em):
    # 1. Provide Status Exception (Not a status report)
    em.update("Project Alpha is on track, but the database migration is riskier than expected.")

    # 2. Flag Cross-Team Issues
    em.escalate("The Ops team keeps rejecting our PRs. Can you align with the Ops EM on their SLA?")

    # 3. Discuss Career Trajectory
    em.ask("I mentored two juniors this month. Does this align with the Staff Engineer promotion rubric?")
```

## 9. Performance Table
How an EM spends their time compared to an IC.

| Activity | IC Time % | EM Time % |
|---|---|---|
| Coding / Architecture | 80% | 5% |
| 1-on-1s / Mentoring | 5% | 40% |
| Cross-team Alignment | 10% | 30% |
| Hiring / Performance | 5% | 25% |

## 10. Top Interview Questions
| Question | Answer |
|---|---|
| How do you manage a low-performing engineer? | I start with clear, actionable feedback. We create a Performance Improvement Plan (PIP) with measurable goals. If they don't improve after coaching, I transition them out to protect the team's morale. |
| How do you handle a conflict between a PM and a Tech Lead? | I act as a mediator. I ensure the PM understands the technical risk (via the TL), and the TL understands the business impact (via the PM). I facilitate a compromise, often involving scoping down the feature. |
| What is the difference between management and leadership? | Management is about processes, budgets, and organization. Leadership is about vision, motivation, and influence. A good EM must do both. |
| How do you measure the success of an EM? | Not by lines of code, but by team retention, predictable delivery velocity, and the career progression of their direct reports. |
| Should an EM write code? | Generally, no. If an EM is on the critical path for a code deliverable, the team is at risk because the EM's schedule is entirely meeting-driven. |

## 11. Tricky Questions & Edge Cases
What if your EM is completely non-technical and doesn't understand your work? Bro, this happens when project managers are promoted to EMs. You must adapt by explaining your blockers entirely in business terms. Don't say, "We have an N+1 query issue." Say, "The current architecture will cause the site to crash on Black Friday." They will understand the latter.

## 12. Real-World Usage
At Google, EMs (often called SWE Managers) are expected to have a strong technical background, but they explicitly rely on Tech Leads (TLs) for architecture decisions. The TL/EM partnership is the foundation of execution at scale.

## 13. Best Practices
| DO | DON'T |
|---|---|
| Create a "brag document" to track your wins for your EM. | Expect your EM to magically know every commit you make. |
| Bring solutions to 1-on-1s, not just complaints. | Treat 1-on-1s as a status report for Jira tickets. |
| Give your EM feedback on their management style. | Suffer in silence if you are burning out. |

## 14. Production Notes
<Admonition type="info" title="The Peter Principle">
Many orgs promote their best engineers into management. This often results in losing a great engineer and gaining a terrible manager. Management is a completely different career track, not a promotion from senior engineer.
</Admonition>

## 15. Common Mistakes
| Mistake | Correction |
|---|---|
| Canceling 1-on-1s because you are "too busy coding" | 1-on-1s are the most important meeting of your week |
| Asking the EM to debug your code | Go to your Tech Lead or a Senior IC |
| Assuming the EM controls product direction | Direct product complaints to the PM |
